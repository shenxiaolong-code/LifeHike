# Outdoor Gear and Survival Gear
- My rule for solo heavy-pack hiking is simple: choose effort over risk. The goal is to build strength, sharpen resilience, and stay grounded, not chase adrenaline.
- I carry survival gear for one reason only: self-rescue if an unexpected off-trail route turns serious. It is insurance, not an invitation to danger.

---
# Outdoor Gear
- 65L backpack + 15L day pack
- Trekking poles (packed as backup, rarely used)
- Headlamp (+ spare LiFePO4 battery)
- Power bank + offline maps
- 3 adhesive bandages

---
# Survival Gear
- 22 m climbing setup (10 mm nylon static rope + D-lock carabiner)
- 19-point crampons
- 2 emergency thermal blankets
- Portable water filter + 1 m silicone tube
- 2 lighters
- Survival whistle
- Basic compass

---
# Timeline
- Over 15 years, I have gone from a fully loaded setup to mostly a single 65L pack, phasing out poles (kept as backup), knee braces, wrist supports, gaiters, gloves, and a sun hat.
- After Laojun Mountain, I added crampons as a safeguard in case an off-trail "mystery route" turns into hazardous ground, such as a reverse traverse of Laojun Mountain.

---
# Proof from Experience
- Heavy-pack hiking does not automatically harm the knees or meniscus. With proper technique, it can make them stronger.
- Fifteen years ago, I came down Zhangjiajie empty-handed and limping. Today, I can run downhill with a light pack, or descend with a heavy pack and no poles, with no knee discomfort. In my experience, smart load management plus sound movement mechanics strengthens the body rather than wearing it down.
